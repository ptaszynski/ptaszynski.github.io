ML-Ask · Affect Analysis System

ML-Ask is a system for affect analysis of textual input in Japanese. It is based on a linguistic assumption that emotional states of a speaker are conveyed by emotional expressions used in emotive utterances. ML-Ask first separates emotive utterances from non-emotive ones, then within the emotive utterances seeks expressions of specific emotion types. Read more →

CAO · Emoticon Analysis System

CAO is a system for analysis of emoticons in Japanese online communication. It extracts emoticons from input and determines the specific emotion types they express. It first matches extracted emoticons against a database of over ten thousand samples; emoticons for which emotion types cannot be determined this way are automatically decomposed into semantic areas representing “mouths” or “eyes”, then annotated according to their co-occurrence in the database. Automatic Evaluation of Conversational Agents

The two systems above are used in further research on enhancing human–computer interaction. This method uses affect analysis as a background procedure during conversations with Japanese-speaking conversational agents; users’ emotional engagement during conversation is reinterpreted as general attitudes toward the agent. Tendencies obtained this way were comparable to results of a post-conversation questionnaire, showing that the method is applicable to automatic evaluation of Japanese-speaking conversational agents. Contextual Appropriateness of Emotions

A novel approach to the analysis of emotional states that determines not only what emotion was expressed, but also whether it was appropriate for the context of the conversation. An affect analysis system estimates the speaker’s affective states and a web-mining technique gathers from the Internet a list of emotions that should be expressed at that moment. Implemented in a conversational agent, this method could differentiate sincere/appropriate expressions from insincere/inappropriate ones (irony, harassment, etc.) and choose appropriate conversational procedures. Automatic Detection of Cyberbullying Activities

Online slandering and bullying have been an urgent problem on unofficial websites of Japanese schools. School personnel and members of Parent-Teacher Associations have started Online Patrol to spot websites and blogs containing such inappropriate content; however, the countless number of such pages makes the job an uphill task. This research develops a systematic approach to Online Patrol by automatically spotting suspicious entries and reporting them to PTA members. The analysis combines SVM-based machine learning with ML-Ask-driven feature analysis to improve the model. Read more →
